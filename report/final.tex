\begin{abstract}
% Provide a concise summary of your project. Your abstract should briefly describe the problem, why it is important, your main approach, key results, and the main conclusion. Note your track here.
Video object segmentation (VOS) is judged almost only by per-frame overlap ($\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$), 
which ignores the temporal consistency a downstream controller relies on. 
We apply \emph{test-time gradient correction}~\citep{li2020cycle} to the AOT~\citep{yang2021aot} pipeline and, 
beyond standard metrics, evaluate every mask sequence in a closed-loop visual-servoing simulator using physical metrics 
(tracking error, control energy, centroid jerk, lock-loss). 
On DAVIS-2017, correction does give $\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$ gain but at a large speed cost, 
yet the servoing harness reveals stability differences the aggregate score hides.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

% Clearly introduce the problem your project addresses and explain why it is important. Provide enough background for readers to understand the motivation, then summarize your project goal, approach, and main contributions.
Semi-supervised video object segmentation (VOS)~\citep{pont2017davis} asks a model to track and segment target objects throughout a video given only a first-frame annotation. Modern memory-based models such as STM~\citep{oh2019stm} and AOT~\citep{yang2021aot} reach high accuracy and are ranked almost exclusively by the per-frame region-and-boundary score $\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$. This metric, however, rewards spatial overlap while remaining largely insensitive to \emph{temporal} behaviour: a mask whose centroid jitters frame-to-frame can score nearly identically to a temporally smooth one. For physical robotics tasks such as image-based visual servoing~\citep{chaumette2006visual}, this distinction is critical, because high-frequency perception noise propagates directly into actuator jerk, wasted control energy, and tracking lock-loss.

To close the gap between perception quality and control stability, we revisit \emph{test-time gradient correction}~\citep{li2020cycle}. At inference time, this technique treats the first-frame ground truth as an anchor and performs a few gradient-descent updates on the current prediction, following the gradient of a cycle-consistency loss that propagates the mask forward and back to frame~1. We integrate this refinement into the AOT~\citep{yang2021aot} inference engine and ask a question that the VOS literature has not posed: does refining masks at test time produce a measurable improvement in downstream \emph{physical} tracking, rather than only in $\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$?

Our contributions are: (i) a faithful re-implementation and audit of AOT~$+$~gradient correction on DAVIS-2017, including diagnostics that reveal why the correction signal is weak in practice and a more stable ``legacy'' variant; (ii) an open-source closed-loop visual-servoing simulator that converts any mask sequence into physically interpretable control metrics (tracking RMSE, $\mathrm{P}99$ error, control energy, centroid jerk, lock-loss); and (iii) an analysis showing that gradient correction gives only a marginal $\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$ gain ($+0.33$, mostly on boundary $\mathcal{F}$) at a large speed cost, while the servoing harness surfaces stability differences that the aggregate metric conceals.

\section{Related Work}

% Discuss the most relevant prior work related to your project. Rather than listing papers one by one, try to organize them by themes, explain their limitations, and clearly describe how your project is related to or different from existing work.
\paragraph{Memory-based VOS architectures.}
The dominant family for semi-supervised VOS maintains an external memory of past frame--mask pairs and retrieves it through attention-based matching. STM~\citep{oh2019stm} introduced this paradigm; AOT~\citep{yang2021aot} added an identification mechanism that segments multiple objects in a single forward pass; DeAOT~\citep{yang2022deaot} decoupled visual and identity features for hierarchical propagation; and XMem~\citep{cheng2022xmem} added a long-term memory pool for minute-long videos. All of these operate purely \emph{offline} at inference, leaving the predicted masks unrefined---precisely the gap that test-time correction targets. We build on AOT as our backbone.

\paragraph{Test-time refinement and cycle consistency.}
Online-learning methods such as OnAVOS~\citep{voigtlaender2017onavos} fine-tune network weights on the first frame; they are accurate but prohibitively slow ($<\!1$ FPS). \citet{li2020cycle} instead keep the weights fixed and use the first-frame ground-truth mask as a self-supervisory anchor, applying \emph{gradient correction}: a few PGD-style updates of the predicted mask along the gradient of a cycle-consistency loss $\mathcal{L}\bigl(S_\theta(X_t,\hat Y^{l}_t,X_1),Y_1\bigr)$. While cycle consistency is well established in image translation, \citet{li2020cycle} is, to our knowledge, the only work to use it as a test-time \emph{control signal} for VOS. Our work sits in this thread but re-examines the technique through a control-theoretic lens and reports its true accuracy--latency trade-off.

\paragraph{Perception for visual servoing.}
Classical image-based visual servoing (IBVS)~\citep{chaumette2006visual} reduces tracking control to driving a per-frame feature error to zero. Modern systems typically rely on bounding-box deep trackers rather than pixel-precise VOS, and rarely analyse how perception errors propagate into control errors. We instead use IBVS as a downstream evaluation harness for VOS masks---an angle absent from the gradient-correction literature---to quantify how mask refinement affects physical tracking stability.

\section{Method / Approach}

% Describe your proposed method or approach in detail. Include the problem formulation, model architecture, algorithms, equations, or technical design when appropriate, and explain why your approach is suitable for the problem.

\subsection{Gradient Correction}

Our backbone is AOT~\citep{yang2021aot}, a memory-based VOS model that, given the first frame $X_1$ and
its ground-truth mask $Y_1$, propagates the target to every later frame $X_t$ by matching against a
memory of past frame--mask pairs. Like all such models, AOT runs purely feed-forward at inference, so a
prediction error at frame $t$ is never corrected and tends to accumulate over time. Gradient
correction~\citep{li2020cycle} addresses this by treating the first-frame ground truth as a
self-supervisory anchor and refining the current prediction at test time, while keeping the network
weights $\theta$ frozen.

The key idea is a \emph{cycle}: if a predicted mask $\hat{Y}_t$ for frame $t$ is correct, then using
$(X_t,\hat{Y}_t)$ as a reference to segment the \emph{first} frame $X_1$ should reproduce the known
ground truth $Y_1$. We therefore feed the current frame back into the same (frozen) segmentation network
$S_\theta$ as a reference, with the soft prediction $\hat{Y}_t$ as its reference mask, and predict the
first frame; the discrepancy with $Y_1$ measures how self-consistent $\hat{Y}_t$ is. Minimizing this
discrepancy with respect to the mask itself yields the gradient-correction update (Eq.~(5) of
\citet{li2020cycle}):
\[
\hat{Y}_t^{(l+1)} \;=\; \operatorname{proj}_{\Delta}\!\left(
\hat{Y}_t^{(l)} \;-\; \alpha\,
\frac{\partial\, \mathcal{L}\!\left(\mathcal{G}\!\left(\mathcal{F}(\hat{Y}_t^{(l)})\right),\, Y_1\right)}
{\partial\, \hat{Y}_t^{(l)}}
\right),
\qquad l = 0,\ldots,N-1,
\]
where $\mathcal{F}$ propagates the soft mask $\hat{Y}_t^{(l)}$ from frame $t$ forward, $\mathcal{G}$
maps it back onto the first frame to form the cyclic reconstruction
$\mathcal{G}(\mathcal{F}(\hat{Y}_t^{(l)}))=S_\theta(X_t,\hat{Y}_t^{(l)},X_1)$, $\alpha$ is the step size,
and $\operatorname{proj}_{\Delta}$ projects each pixel back onto the probability simplex so that the mask
stays a valid soft one-hot distribution over object IDs. The mask is kept as a soft distribution throughout, which makes
the whole cycle differentiable so the gradient can flow back to $\hat{Y}_t^{(l)}$.

The cyclic loss $\mathcal{L}$ compares the reconstructed first frame against the true first-frame mask
$Y_1$ and combines a cross-entropy term with a soft-IoU term,
\[
\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}\!\left(\hat{Y}_1, Y_1\right)
            + \tfrac{1}{2}\left(1 - \mathrm{IoU}_{\text{soft}}\!\left(\hat{Y}_1, Y_1\right)\right),
\qquad \hat{Y}_1 = \mathcal{G}\!\left(\mathcal{F}(\hat{Y}_t^{(l)})\right),
\]
where the cross-entropy enforces correct per-pixel ID assignment and the soft-IoU term directly rewards
region overlap. After $N$ inner steps the corrected mask $\hat{Y}_t^{(N)}$ replaces the raw prediction
and is written into AOT's memory, so the refinement also benefits the propagation of subsequent frames.

Our implementation has three hyperparameters: the correction interval $K$ (apply the
update every $K$ frames), the number of inner steps $N$, and the step size $\alpha$. We found that a
faithful, end-to-end differentiable cycle produces very small gradients ($\lVert g\rVert\!\sim\!10^{-3}$),
so a single conservative step barely moves the mask, whereas a large $\alpha$ overshoots and degrades
easy sequences. We correct \emph{every} frame ($K{=}1$) with $N{=}20$ inner steps; the simplex projection keeps each update bounded. This setting gives
the best $\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$ on DAVIS-2017 val among the configurations we swept, at the cost of a substantially lower inference speed.

\subsection{Visual-Servoing Simulator}

After obtaining a refined model, we need to verify whether the refinement improves a physically meaningful
 downstream task, not only the standard $\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$ scores. For this purpose, we build a 
 lightweight image-based visual-servoing simulator. The simulator treats each predicted mask sequence 
 as the perception module of a closed-loop tracking system: the mask provides the object position, a 
 state estimator smooths the noisy observation, and a virtual pan--tilt camera is controlled to keep 
 the object centered. This design is suitable for our problem because temporal mask jitter, missing masks,
  and small centroid shifts are converted into control metrics such as tracking error, control effort, 
  and centroid jerk.

The input to the simulator is a DAVIS-style indexed PNG mask sequence, where pixel value $0$ denotes 
background and positive integer values denote object IDs. For each frame $t$, we extract the centroid 
observation
\[
z_t = (c_x, c_y) =
\left(\frac{1}{|\Omega_t|}\sum_{(x,y)\in\Omega_t} x,\;
\frac{1}{|\Omega_t|}\sum_{(x,y)\in\Omega_t} y\right),
\quad
\Omega_t=\{(x,y): M_t(x,y)=\texttt{obj\_id}\}.
\]
If the object is absent, the observation is marked as missing and the estimator performs prediction only. 
The estimator is a constant-velocity Kalman filter with state
\[
x_t = [c_x,\; c_y,\; v_x,\; v_y]^\top,
\]
which reduces high-frequency centroid noise while preserving object motion. The virtual camera position, 
denoted by $p_t$, follows the filtered centroid using a discrete PID controller. At each frame, the 
control error is
\[
e_t = \hat{x}_{t,1:2} - p_t,
\]
and the command is
\[
u_t = K_p e_t + K_i \sum_{\tau \leq t} e_\tau \Delta t
      + K_d \frac{e_t-e_{t-1}}{\Delta t},
\qquad
p_{t+1}=p_t+u_t\Delta t.
\]
Our visual-servoing evaluation pipeline is shown in Figure \ref{fig:servo-flow}. In our experiments, we 
use the default DAVIS frame rate of $24$ FPS, a constant-velocity Kalman model, and PID gains 
$(K_p,K_i,K_d)=(0.45,0,0.05)$.

\begin{figure}[h]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figs/servo_flow.png}
  \caption{Visual-servoing evaluation pipeline.}
  \label{fig:servo-flow}
\end{figure}

The core simulation loop can be summarized as:
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
\textbf{Input:} & \text{mask sequence } \{M_t\}_{t=1}^{T},\; \text{object id},\; \Delta t \\
\textbf{Initialize:} & z_1 \leftarrow \text{first available centroid},\quad
                     \hat{x}_1 \leftarrow [z_1,0,0],\quad p_1 \leftarrow z_1 \\
\textbf{For } t=1,\ldots,T: &
  z_t \leftarrow \text{centroid}(M_t,\text{object id}) \\
& \hat{x}_t \leftarrow \text{KalmanPredictUpdate}(z_t) \\
& e_t \leftarrow \hat{x}_{t,1:2}-p_t \\
& u_t \leftarrow \text{PID}(e_t) \\
& p_{t+1} \leftarrow p_t + u_t\Delta t \\
& \text{log } z_t,\hat{x}_t,e_t,u_t,p_t
\end{array}
\]

In the process, the script reports tracking RMSE, 99th-percentile tracking error, normalized 
tracking error, total control energy, centroid jerk RMS, lock-loss frames, 
and missing-frame counts. The \texttt{single} mode compares arbitrary mask sources on one sequence and 
saves diagnostic plots, while the \texttt{all} mode aggregates AOT and AOT+GC over the DAVIS-2017 
validation set into \texttt{per\_sequence.csv}, \texttt{summary.json}, and summary figures. The detailed 
visualizations and numerical results are presented in the next section.

\section{Results \& Analysis}

We evaluate AOT+GC against the original AOT output on the DAVIS-2017 validation sequences using the
visual-servoing simulator described above. For each sequence, the simulator tracks the selected object
centroid from the indexed mask sequence and reports downstream control metrics. Since lower values are
better for all metrics in this section, a positive improvement means that AOT+GC produced a lower value
than AOT(ori). The main takeaway is conservative: gradient correction produces only very small average
changes, but it tends to move tracking error and control effort in the right direction on more sequences
than it hurts.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \toprule
    Metric & AOT(ori) mean & AOT+GC mean & AOT+GC win / tie / loss \\
    \midrule
    Tracking RMSE (px) & 89.218 & 89.196 & 20 / 5 / 5 \\
    Tracking P99 (px) & 156.611 & 156.553 & 20 / 3 / 7 \\
    Control energy & 3310.291 & 3309.868 & 19 / 6 / 5 \\
    Centroid jerk RMS & 11590.960 & 11591.217 & 15 / 1 / 14 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{Visual-servoing metrics on the 30 evaluated DAVIS-2017 validation sequences.}
  \label{tab:servo-results}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:servo-results} shows that the aggregate means are almost unchanged. AOT+GC slightly
reduces tracking RMSE, 99th-percentile tracking error, and control energy, but the relative gains are
only about $0.02\%$. Centroid jerk RMS is even less stable: the per-sequence average improvement is
close to zero, while the aggregate mean is slightly worse for AOT+GC. This suggests that test-time
correction does not reliably smooth the centroid trajectory, even when it improves the object position
used by the controller.

Figure~\ref{fig:servo-win-mean} clarifies the difference between magnitude and consistency. AOT+GC wins
on $76.67\%$ of sequences for tracking RMSE, $66.67\%$ for tracking P99, and $83.33\%$ for control
energy. These win rates indicate a mild positive downstream effect, especially for the controller's
energy use. However, the average improvement bars remain close to zero, so the method should not be
described as producing a large control improvement. Missing frames and missing rate are unchanged: only
the \texttt{india} sequence has missing detections, and both methods miss the same 13 of 81 frames.

The per-sequence improvement plots support a sequence-dependent interpretation. Some videos receive a
clearer downstream benefit from correction, while most videos have nearly identical AOT(ori) and AOT+GC
servoing behavior and a few are slightly worse. This is why we did not include raw metric-comparison
figures whose axes mix very different scales: tracking error would be visually compressed by larger
jerk and control-energy values. We also omit missing-frame boxplots because almost all values are zero
and they do not add meaningful evidence. Overall, the visual-servoing simulator complements standard
DAVIS $\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$ evaluation by exposing downstream control stability, even when the mask
quality differences are too small to be visually obvious in ordinary VOS metrics.

\section{Conclusion}

This project integrates test-time gradient correction into the AOT video object segmentation pipeline
and evaluates the refined masks from both perception and downstream-control perspectives. In addition
to the standard DAVIS $\mathcal{J}\&\mathcal{F}$ score, we built and used a visual-servoing simulator to
measure tracking error, tail error, control energy, centroid jerk, and missing-frame behavior. This
second evaluation view is useful because temporally small mask changes can affect controller stability
even when aggregate VOS metrics look almost unchanged.

The final results are mixed but informative. AOT+GC gives only limited average improvement, yet it
slightly improves tracking error and control energy on a majority of evaluated sequences. The centroid
jerk result is not stable, and missing-frame behavior is essentially unchanged. These findings suggest
that gradient correction can be directionally helpful for downstream servoing, but the current version is
not strong or consistent enough to claim a large practical gain.

The main limitations are the high inference cost of test-time optimization, the very small average
improvement, unstable behavior across sequences, and visualizations that are still mostly metric-level
rather than trajectory-level. Future work should investigate selective correction only on uncertain
frames, adaptive step sizes, stronger temporal-consistency losses, trajectory-overlay or JPEG-projection
visualizations, and more realistic camera dynamics in the simulator.
